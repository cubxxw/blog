# 2025 Annual Review

## 📅 Hi! 2026 ~

Before writing this, I flipped through my notes from December 28, 2025.

That day I wrote: "There's nothing much to write for this year's annual review either. In the end, I walked my own path, but... let's just treat it as a game."

I genuinely felt there was nothing to write at the time. Looking back now, I realize it was because too much had happened — so much that it was hard to know where to start, or rather, so much that I needed a little distance to see clearly what I had actually experienced.

So this piece is written in retrospect. Written for the me in 2026, and also for that person sitting somewhere at the end of December, a little lost, but not yet ready to give up.

---

## A Stone That Keeps Being Submerged

This year, I was like a stone that kept being dropped into water.

Every time I entered a new city, I soaked in its waters — absorbing its temperature, its colors, its smells — and came out tinted with something new, before being lifted out and dropped into the next body of water.

Korea, Shenzhen, Hội An, Ho Chi Minh City, Kansai, Kyoto, Osaka, Yakushima, Kumano... and several cities across China. I'm not saying this to boast about the numbers. I want to describe the texture of this kind of life, and there's really no better word for it than this: a stone, soaking in different waters, each time it emerges carrying new patterns etched into it.

But the problem with being a stone is: after being submerged for so long, you sometimes can't tell which layer of color is yours, and which belongs to the water.

## The Old Man Who Kept His Shop for 40 Years

July. Lake Kawaguchiko, Japan.

A friend and I walked into a small local restaurant. The owner, a man named Watanabe, was seventy years old. His face was carved with wrinkles, and he had been running this place for nearly four decades. The restaurant was called "Non Hei のん兵."

We got to talking. He said: "When I was young, I worked in Tokyo. Every day I was just a cog in someone else's machine. Eventually I thought — this is no way for a person to live. Here, I can see Mount Fuji. I can see the smiles on my customers' faces. I can feel the changing of the seasons."

He also said: "Flavor is something that lives in a customer's memory. I can't just change it on a whim. That would be a betrayal."

Midway through the meal, he quietly brought out a side dish we hadn't ordered, put his finger to his lips — a gesture to keep it between us. Two Western tourists nearby caught the whole scene and were quietly grinning.

When we left, he walked us out himself, and with a very serious expression, made sure we checked that we hadn't forgotten any of our belongings. That gravity was somehow endearing.

I didn't sit in that restaurant for very long, but I've thought about it many times since. Not because the food was especially good, but because Watanabe's forty years made me realize something: "slow" isn't laziness, and "deep" isn't being stuck in the past. He was doing the same thing every day, but each day was accumulating something — a relationship with this land, with these dishes, with the travelers passing through. That was something I was missing very much at the time.

This year I encountered several people like that.

An elderly woman at a Korean bus stop who, despite the language barrier, pulled out a bottle of beer and handed it to me, then knocked back her own in one go — that kind of effortless openness toward a stranger.

Archer and friends in Shenzhen, where I stayed for ten days. Every day there was food, there were people, there was someone who knew where you'd gone that day. It wasn't until I left that I realized: those ten days, I was being caught by something. And I had needed that very, very much.

A café in Hội An, Vietnam, staffed entirely by deaf young people. The owner had turned down opportunities in the US to come back and build a place where these kids could have a corner of the world to call their own during their youth. The entire wall was covered in sticky notes from visitors around the globe. I sat in the corner for a long time. When I walked out, my throat felt tight.

On a street in Hội An, a small group of young Russians, all working on MacBooks, having fled a closing country. Their situation was nothing like mine, but I recognized something in their eyes — that familiar thing of using "leaving" to cope with some kind of "can't go back."

None of these people I will ever see again. But they all stayed — not as memories exactly, but as reference points for the question: *what is real?*

## Slightly Drunk Me Became an INFP

May 26th. Five in the morning. I wrote a few hundred of the most honest words I'd written all year.

I'd had a bit to drink the night before. Fell asleep past midnight. Woke at five on my own — alert, senses amplified. Sunlight came through the window, carrying leaf-shaped shadows onto the wall. I ordered delivery. The soy milk tasted extraordinary; the steamed bun tasted terrible.

Then I noticed something was off. That "can't quite name it" kind of off.

I lay back down, thought I'd sleep for an hour, set an alarm — but couldn't sleep. My subconscious started guilting me: can't sleep means wasting time. Then I caught myself doing that. So I canceled the alarm, let the runaway thoughts keep running, told myself: "Feel a bit like crying? Then cry."

I wrote in Flomo: "What I love is the process of self-growth, of understanding myself better — not meaning itself."

Then I went to the park. I wanted to lie on the grass, but found it covered in dew. Went home, got a sleeping pad, then went back.

That entry became a kind of undertone for this whole piece. That morning taught me something: amplified emotions aren't weakness, they're signals. That was the body saying: *you haven't really processed what's in there*. The slight intoxication just temporarily thinned the membrane I usually keep in place with rationality.

That morning, I became an INFP. Not in the personality-test sense — in the experiential sense. That state where "everything is real, including the fact that the steamed bun tastes terrible."

## The World Started Repeating

In December, I wrote something genuinely honest:

> The exploration itself no longer gives me enough pull. In the early days of nomadic living, three things overlapped: the unfamiliarity of space, a loosening of identity, an expanding sense of possibility. But more than a year in, the world began to repeat. Cities change, but their underlying structure is the same. I adapt faster and faster; the tremors get smaller and smaller. Going somewhere no longer automatically means I'll be changed.

Saying that out loud feels a little like a confession, but it's true.

Nomadic living has a tipping point. Past that point, "unfamiliarity" transforms from something that opens you up into something you're already very familiar with. You know the excitement of the first three days in a new city. You know the second week you'll start looking for a regular café. You know the third week you'll start wanting to leave.

This isn't failure. It's a structural shift.

One morning in Hội An, sitting in Rosie's Café, eating brunch, staring out the window. I noticed my attention had dropped — not occasionally, but continuously, structurally.

I made a diagnostic chart: rhythm disrupted, environmental cues gone, tasks undefined, too many novel distractions...

After finishing it, I stared at it for a long time. What it came down to was: this is a *fulcrum* problem. When movement becomes your main axis, you need something unmoving to serve as a fulcrum. Movement without a fulcrum is just changing backgrounds.

By the end of 2025, I still hadn't found that fulcrum.

## A Cosmic Monologue

Traveling alone, thinking alone — but what exactly am I thinking about?

I went through all my Flomo entries from 2025, trying to answer that question.

There was one thread that kept appearing, just wearing different disguises: **In an age where AI grows ever more capable, how does a person maintain their own subjecthood?**

This question appeared through Watanabe — he refused to change his recipes because he knew what he was guarding.

It appeared through the deaf café in Hội An — every cup of coffee there was made by a specific person using specific hands, an act with zero efficiency advantage, but that was precisely the point.

It appeared through the young Russians — how does a person maintain an open inner core inside an increasingly closed system?

It appeared through that slightly drunk morning — what I love is "the process of understanding myself better," not meaning itself.

It appeared through the financial crisis at year's end — I lost some things, but the thread of "who am I" didn't break.

So this is what all the solo traveling and solo thinking amounted to:

If you strip away all the locations, the projects, the relationships — what is left of the "me"?

I haven't found a complete answer. But I'm growing increasingly clear on what it isn't: it's not in any particular city, not in any successful project, not in any relationship. It lives in the moments that can't be optimized — the fact that the steamed bun tasted terrible, the grandmother's beer, the gravity on Watanabe's face as he walked us out the door.

## The Tao Has Only One Form; AI Doesn't Change It

This year, my relationship with AI was deeper, more everyday, and more clear-eyed than any year before.

When DeepSeek R1 dropped, I spent a long time thinking about what remains for knowledge workers in the age of AI. My conclusion: not information itself, but the subject who processes information. What AI delivers is the statistical common denominator — perfect, secondhand. Only the part you yourself filter and reconstruct carries genuine subjecthood.

But I also discovered: AI changed me a lot, and also didn't change anything core.

What it changed: speed, the way I acquire information, the efficiency with which I build knowledge frameworks. I used AI to explore the mythology of Angkor Wat, to research the history of the Kumano Kodo, to organize product logic. These are real accelerations.

What it didn't change: *why* I walked the Kumano Kodo — that motivation is mine. Being moved in Watanabe's restaurant — that feeling is mine. The tears in that slightly drunk morning — they're mine. AI can help me analyze *why* I was moved, but it can't substitute for the being-moved itself.

Wang Yangming said: "Unity of knowing and doing." Knowing and doing are two different things; the distance between them is called experience, feeling, what you've ground out for yourself in reality. No tool can shorten that.

The Tao has only one form. No tool changes it. Tools change *how* you walk, not *where* you're headed.

This year, AI was my best tool. But I'm also increasingly clear: it's ultimately a tool. The one who holds the tool, who decides which path to take — that has to be me.

## Pain Is the Only Teacher That Doesn't Lie

Late December. Cash flow hit a wall.

I remember the moment I saw that number. Not panic — a very particular kind of "full helplessness." You look at it, you know it's real, you know you have to face it, but for a moment you don't know where to begin.

I wrote afterward: "Even though I could have predicted this earlier, actually facing a problem like this — I'm completely lost."

Throughout this year, "gamifying" things was my most-used mental framework. Financial problems become level bosses in the game. Nomadic living becomes map exploration. Every time trouble hits, it's this round's challenge. This framework has genuine value — it let me keep moving forward with a certain lightness, instead of collapsing every time something went wrong.

But at the moment of the financial crisis, the game framework briefly failed.

Not because this was objectively worse than other things, but because that "full helplessness" doesn't allow you to process it with a framework. You just have to sit there and feel it.

That moment I realized: pain is the only teacher that doesn't lie.

Joy can be performed. Calm can be maintained. Even the "gamified mindset" can be a protective mechanism. But genuine helplessness can't be routed around. It tells you with absolute honesty: your approach to this thing has a problem. You need to take it seriously now.

The deepest growth of this year was hidden in those moments of "full helplessness." Not in the easy days.

## Let's Call It a Game — A Closing Note

Next year starts from zero.

But I want to say something about the phrase "let's just treat it as a game," because I don't want it to be misread.

It's not avoidance. It's not numbness. It's not "nothing matters anyway."

A game has rules, has risks, has costs, has rewards. To "treat something as a game" isn't to dismiss all of that — it's to accept it while maintaining a state where you're not entirely defined by the outcome.

This year I lost some things, won some others. Cash flow fell apart, but I was genuinely caught in Shenzhen. The exploration started to repeat, but on the Kumano Kodo I walked into a real kind of silence. I worked on products, but I met Watanabe, met the old man at Lake Kawaguchiko who secretly brought out that side dish.

At Ryōan-ji there is a stone water basin engraved with: 「吾唯足知」— "I only know what is enough."

Not a call to be satisfied with things as they are. A reminder to know what you have.

This year, I came to know some things.

I know that movement can be a protective mechanism, and can also be an entry point for growth — simultaneously.

I know that being caught by someone is a need, not a weakness.

I know AI is a tool, but the tool doesn't decide where you're going.

I know that "full helplessness" holds something worth taking seriously.

I know that Watanabe's kind of "slow" and "deep" is something I want to move toward, even though I don't yet know how to get there.

Put together, this isn't victory and it isn't defeat. It's one save point in the game.

Hey, 2026.

I'm here.
